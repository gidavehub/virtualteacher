**16. SCRIPT**

**17. SCRIPT (Giga story)**
The Giga Initiative, by UNICEF and ITU, has connected schools around the world. In other countries, students who once studied by candlelight now code, create, and compete globally. For example, in Sierra Leone, just a few years ago, connecting a single school to the internet could cost as much as twelve thousand dollars per year. Twelve thousand dollars… for one school. That meant most schools simply could not afford to stay connected. But with the support of Giga, things have changed. By working with governments and the private sector, by improving procurement, increasing competition, and using new technologies, the cost dropped — from twelve thousand to just fifteen hundred dollars per school, per year. It dropped nearly ninety percent! This changed everything. Schools that were once offline started to come online. It's not just about connecting a school — it's about making sure that connections last. In Kakuma refugee camp in Kenya — a place where over two hundred and fifty thousand people are building their lives after fleeing conflict — a girl named Darlene is learning to code. She wants to become a software engineer. And she is not alone. Her classmates are building websites, solving problems, imagining a future far beyond the camp. This became possible because their school is connected. Across Kenya, Giga has helped connect six hundred and fifty-nine schools, reaching more than four hundred and twenty-five thousand students and teachers. When connectivity is done right, it becomes hope. And here in The Gambia? The Vice President signed our letter of interest in May, this year. That kicked off a nationwide mapping exercise — and now every single one of the 1,978 schools is on the map. Data from health facilities and TVET institutions is being reviewed to add to the map, too. Discussions are underway to connect the unconnected facilities in ways that would reduce both upfront and ongoing costs. The national ICT infrastructure is being assessed. The policies are being reviewed. The National Steering Committee is coming together. There you go, class — we have done the mapping. We have done the planning. What we need now is the doing. Think about that. Oh, it's time for another break. Enjoy your meal. When you come back to me, we are going to talk about something a little more serious. But it is very important.
